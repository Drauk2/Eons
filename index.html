Life of the Buddha

Siddhartha Gautama, who would become the Buddha, was born in the 5th century BCE in what is now Nepal. Born a prince, he lived a life of luxury but was moved by the suffering he saw outside the palace walls.

At the age of 29, he left his palace and family to seek enlightenment. After years of ascetic practices and meditation, he attained enlightenment under the Bodhi tree at Bodh Gaya.

The Buddha then spent the next 45 years teaching the path to enlightenment throughout northeastern India.

Sacred Texts of Buddhism

Buddhist scriptures are extensive and varied, with each tradition having its own collection of texts.

The Pali Canon (Tipitaka) is the earliest collection of Buddhist scriptures, preserved by the Theravada tradition. It consists of three "baskets": the Vinaya Pitaka (monastic rules), Sutta Pitaka (discourses), and Abhidhamma Pitaka (philosophical analysis).

Mahayana Sutras include well-known texts like the Lotus Sutra, Heart Sutra, and Diamond Sutra, which emphasize the bodhisattva path and emptiness (shunyata).

Buddhist Meditation Practices

Meditation is central to Buddhist practice, with two main types found across traditions:

Samatha (calm abiding) meditation develops concentration and mindfulness through focusing on an object, often the breath.

Vipassana (insight) meditation cultivates awareness of the true nature of reality, particularly the three marks of existence: impermanence, suffering, and non-self.

Different Buddhist traditions have developed various meditation techniques, including Zen sitting meditation, Tibetan visualizations, and loving-kindness (metta) meditation.

Theravada Buddhism

Theravada, meaning "Way of the Elders," is the oldest surviving Buddhist school. It is predominant in Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, Laos, and Cambodia.

Theravada emphasizes the original teachings of the Buddha as preserved in the Pali Canon. The ideal figure is the arhat, one who has attained nirvana through their own efforts.

Practice focuses on the Noble Eightfold Path, meditation, and monastic discipline. Laypeople support the monastic community and practice generosity and morality.

Mahayana Buddhism

Mahayana, or the "Great Vehicle," emerged around the 1st century BCE and spread throughout East Asia and to Tibet.

Major Mahayana schools include Zen, Pure Land, Nichiren, and Tiantai. Mahayana is predominant in China, Japan, Korea, Vietnam, and Tibet.

Vajrayana Buddhism

Vajrayana, or the "Diamond Vehicle," is often considered a form of Mahayana Buddhism but with distinctive practices. It is predominant in Tibet, Bhutan, and Mongolia.

Vajrayana incorporates tantric practices, including deity yoga, mantras, mandalas, and advanced meditation techniques. It emphasizes the possibility of achieving enlightenment in a single lifetime.

This tradition is known for its rich symbolism, complex rituals, and the importance of the guru-disciple relationship. The Dalai Lama is the most well-known figure of Tibetan Buddhism.
